Research
Introduction
The creation of new economic value is crucial for the founding and growth of new firms and the ongoing survival of incumbents. Startups are an important source of innovative products and services, novel ways of organizing, and creative go-to-market strategies. Within established firms, developing entrepreneurial capabilities are consistently and widely regarded by executives as both a top priority and a tremendous organizational challenge. My research focuses on what catalyzes and suppresses the creation of new value both inside and outside organizations, often challenging widely accepted ideas in entrepreneurship and strategy by highlighting taken-for-granted notions that are incomplete or misleading.
Specifically, I have sought to be a leader in advancing the theoretical development of entrepreneurship, and in particular the nature of entrepreneurial opportunity and the origins of value, entrepreneurial action and the entrepreneurial process, and the connections between value creation and capture (i.e., “strategic entrepreneurship”).
My research contributes to several fields, in particular entrepreneurship and strategic management. I gain purchase on research questions using insights from these fields, along with foundational theories in economics, cognitive science, and analytic philosophy. My aspiration is that my work changes how we think about what entrepreneurship is, what entrepreneurs do, and how institutions and stakeholders shape the entrepreneurial process. For example, I argue and show that policies that foster high-growth entrepreneurship are different from those that spawn small businesses. My work calls into question the assumption that institutions that make it easier to start new companies are unambiguously beneficial; and that common dogma about the entrepreneurial process such as business experimentation, team formation, and gathering feedback from potential customers are not blanket recommendations, but rather strategic decisions that depend crucially on the idea and context.
My theoretical contributions include such concepts as institutional influences on firm formation, the nature of entrepreneurial opportunity, and how the particular types of rents implied by the realization of an entrepreneurial opportunity inform critical early decisions in the venturing process. I contribute methodologically by showing how to use fieldwork, surveys, conjoint analysis, and agent-based modeling to isolate and/or simulate important aspects of entrepreneurial decision-making.
What remains consistent across my various projects is an abiding interest in entrepreneurship of all types—high-growth, replicative, and informal—and the entrepreneurial process as enacted across various organizational and cultural contexts.
Entrepreneurial Opportunity and the Origins of Economic Value
A fundamental question in the field of entrepreneurship is the nature of entrepreneurial opportunity; or, more broadly, what is value and where does it come from? The fields of strategy, organizations, and management largely circumvent this question in much of their theorizing by treating valuable resources, transactions, markets, and firms as given. The field of entrepreneurship has largely focused on how actors get from opportunity to outcome, implicitly taking opportunity as given. In a series of articles (Alvarez, Barney, McBride, & Wuebker, 2014, 2017; McBride & Wuebker, 2022; Stenholm, Acs, & Wuebker, 2013; Welter, Mauer, & Wuebker, 2016; Westgren & Wuebker, 2019; Wuebker, Zenger, & Felin, 2022) my co-authors and I explore the nature of entrepreneurial opportunity and its implications for research, policy, and the classroom.
In Social Objectivity and Entrepreneurial Opportunity: Implications for Entrepreneurship and Management (Academy of Management Review), McBride and Wuebker (2022) contribute to a longstanding debate in entrepreneurship on whether profit opportunities are discovered or encountered by economic actors, or whether value is endogenously created through entrepreneurial action. We first distinguish between the physical world (composed of particles in fields of force, with an independent existence) and the social world (composed of human social institutions, held together by collective agreement). We then show that much of the prior work in entrepreneurship—both the theories that drive the field’s program of research and studies that explore the practices entrepreneurs use such as experimentation and learning—conflate the two. The paper shows that a clearer understanding of the nature of entrepreneurial opportunity resolves several challenges that have bedeviled theory development in prior work, and offers the field a path forward for a future program of research.
On Opportunities: Philosophical and Empirical Implications (Academy of Management Review) considers the objections of scholars who wish to remain committed to the independent existence of entrepreneurial opportunities. Alvarez et al., (2017) ask: Is there a theoretical mechanism available to those who maintain that entrepreneurial opportunities have an independent existence, and that the business of entrepreneurs is to alertly scan the environment and discover them? One of the most common objections to the “creation view” of entrepreneurship by those that advocate for the “discovery view” is that arguing for the endogenous formation of opportunity is tantamount to holding a relativist, socially-constructed view of reality. This paper shows that attempts to bat away the “creation view” of opportunity by pitting critical realism against social constructionism demonstrates a basic misunderstanding of both perspectives, and as a result mishandles the question.
In a related paper, Realism in the Study of Entrepreneurship: A Response (Academy of Management Review), Alvarez et al., (2014) demonstrate that the various theoretical approaches that underpin the “discovery view” in entrepreneurship have their roots in realist and positivist traditions imported from prior work in the philosophy of science. This paper shows that taking the intellectual heritage of the positivist/realist position seriously leads to internally inconsistent conclusions and, for entrepreneurship scholars, unpalatable implications for the field’s program of research. Fortunately, the theories that scholars in the “discovery” tradition have used to buttress their theorizing in prior work have been supplanted by advances in analytic philosophy and the philosophy of science. This paper shows that these advances hold great promise for upgrading our understanding of entrepreneurial opportunity. We explore one of these conceptual advances in detail —social ontology—and show how it can be fruitfully applied to the question of created versus discovered opportunities in entrepreneurship.
Within the “creation view” of entrepreneurship there are three broad and related theoretical perspectives—opportunity creation, effectuation theory, and bricolage. Each has evolved largely distinct from one another, with its own conceptual and empirical tradition. In Bridging Behavioral Models and Theoretical Concepts: Effectuation and Bricolage in the Opportunity Creation Framework (Strategic Entrepreneurship Journal), Welter et. al., (2016) focus attention on the roots of each of these theoretical perspectives and their underlying assumptions and organizes them within a unified conceptual framework. Analysis of this framework offers broad insight about the “creation view” of entrepreneurship as a whole, reveals implications for integrated theory development, and suggests several promising avenues for new research.
Taken as a whole, this collection of papers offer an integrated, internally-consistent view of the underlying ontology of entrepreneurial opportunity along with a set of implications about a program of research and teaching. These papers have contributed to an active debate in the literature, participating in the dialogue and shaping the conversation.
Pressing further, is “entrepreneurial opportunity” really a monolithic construct, as conceptualized in prior work? In An Economic Model of Strategic Entrepreneurship (Strategic Entrepreneurship Journal), Westgren and Wuebker (2019) connect the literature on economic rents in strategy to theories of opportunity in entrepreneurship. We decompose the eponymous “entrepreneurial rent” into four distinct potential rent streams, and then consider the implications for entrepreneurship.  In a formal model of entrepreneurial entry, stakeholders (including, but not limited to, the founder/entrepreneur) can capture the value of an opportunity as it is converted to rents associated with arbitrage, innovation, organization and uncertainty-bearing: essentially, capturing four of the most influential theoretical perspectives in entrepreneurship in one integrated model. Examining these perspectives on value creation together reveals implications for how best to organize value creation and capture throughout the entrepreneurial process, and to conceptualize value capture as a set of distinct rent streams available to multiple stakeholders over time.
In Exploring Country-Level Institutional Arrangements on the Rate and Type of Entrepreneurial Activity (Journal of Business Venturing), Stenholm, Acs and Wuebker (2013) take a high-altitude view of entrepreneurial opportunity by considering how entrepreneurship policy shapes what kinds of opportunities entrepreneurs choose to pursue. Despite decades of sustained scholarly inquiry, the field still struggles to explain why rates of entrepreneurial activity vary widely across regions and countries. Traditional explanations have tended to focus on the influence of entrepreneurial framework conditions on firm creation. Empirical tests of this conjecture have produced mixed, muddled results, and attempts to implement ‘entrepreneur-friendly’ policies in nations, regions, and cities have produced unexpected (and often undesired) outcomes. This paper contributes to the literature on institutions and entrepreneurship by showing that various framework conditions and policy have differential effects on the rate of entrepreneurial activity and what type of entrepreneurial firms emerge. Crucially, we show that policies and institutional support structures that focus on high-growth entrepreneurship can suppress more ordinary, workaday entrepreneurial activity and that policy interventions that attempt to promote entrepreneurship more broadly can suppress the formation and growth of high impact entrepreneurial firms. This paper has influenced the scholarly conversation on institutions and entrepreneurship, and has also influenced entrepreneurship policy-making.
Entrepreneurial Theories (Strategic Management Journal, second round) explores an adjacent thread associated with entrepreneurial opportunity. Wuebker, Zenger and Felin (2022) show that the the attributes of the opportunity that an actor intends to pursue have crucial implications for early organizing and the overall entrepreneurial process. Entrepreneurs face a host of early choices that have significant long-term consequences for the ventures they found. For example, entrepreneurs must decide what form of financing to use, which assets to purchase or employ, whether to keep their ideas secret or not, and what type of experimentation to engage in. This paper argues that these and many other crucial choices entrepreneurs face in the earliest stages of the entrepreneurial process depend critically on the nature of the entrepreneurial idea or theory they plan to pursue. While existing work tends to offer blanket recommendations about these entrepreneurial choices, this paper develops a contingent theory that enables entrepreneurs to appropriately match their theories of value with the appropriate forms of entrepreneurial action.
Entrepreneurial Action and the Entrepreneurial Process
Another stream of research focuses attention on the particulars of the entrepreneurial process, in particular the acquisition of financial resources needed for growth and the search and validation processes associated with early-stage ventures searching for product-market fit. Many of these papers find their origins in the entrepreneurship courses I teach.
I aspire to have entrepreneurship courses that are unabashedly and firmly evidence-based. However, I realized that for both entrepreneurship faculty and the committed founder or founding team the evidence-based insight or practical frameworks they craved were surprisingly thin on the ground. Even the most basic questions—how should I think about composing my founding team? Should I incorporate early, or later? What organizational form is best? Should I release a “minimum viable product” to get market feedback, and what is “minimum enough”—could not be answered definitively. More worryingly, much of what passed for cutting-edge entrepreneurship classroom activities tended to have their origins in practice, and were installed and taught in the classroom as general-purpose approaches despite a lack of scholarly work providing evidence of generality or efficacy. One way to conceptualize a great deal of my early work would be to see those papers as attempting to address the most common questions founders have about what to do in the early days of the entrepreneurial process.
For example, in Delaware Incorporation Matters for New Ventures: Evidence From Venture Capital Investment and the Going-Public Process (Venture Capital), Waisman, Wang, and Wuebker (2009) explore whether incorporating in Delaware benefits high-growth startups. The paper uses a large sample of privately-held companies to investigate the implications of Delaware incorporation on access to venture capital and the implications for the going-public process. We find that companies incorporated in Delaware receive more venture financing and attract more involvement from a broader range of venture investors than startups incorporated elsewhere. We also find that these firms are more likely to go public, get to a liquidity event faster, and generate more exit value than firms incorporated elsewhere. Overall, this study reveals the first empirical evidence about the importance of state laws to privately held, informationally opaque firms seeking venture capital support.
Strategic Alliances by Venture Capital Backed Firms: An Empirical Examination (Small Business Economics) probes deeper into the relationship between startups and investors. Prior work argues that, in addition to providing capital, venture capitalists also add value to entrepreneurial firms by (among other things) providing monitoring and guidance, assisting with sourcing talent, et cetera. Wang, Wuebker, Han, and Ensley, (2012) contribute to the alliances and entrepreneurship literature by showing that strategic alliances are an important and under-studied value-added activity provided by venture capital firms. We find that venture capital firms use alliances as a substitute for financial resources, and that the size of the venture capital syndicate investing in the entrepreneurial firm predicts alliance characteristics. These findings lend additional support to the perspective that alliances represent an important mechanism through which venture capital firms add value to their portfolio companies, and offer insight to startups about the relationship between the idea they exploit, the risks they face, and the financing strategy they pursue.
Another collection of papers (Bigelow, Lundmark, McLean Parks and Wuebker, 2014; Wuebker, Hampl and Wuestenhagen, 2015;  Klein, Wuebker and Zoeller, 2016; Anhokin, Morgan, Schulze and Wuebker, 2022) focus attention on a crucial period in the entrepreneurial process—a startup’s search for the financial resources needed for survival and growth. In particular, these papers focus on the frictions that can prevent entrepreneurial firms from garnering these resources and ultimately realizing their  potential. More broadly, these papers use the investor-entrepreneur relationship as a petri dish to draw out insights for entrepreneurship, along with broader implications about how economic actors make decisions under uncertainty.
Skirting the Issues: Experimental Evidence of Gender Bias in IPO Prospectus Evaluations (Journal of Management) and The Strength of Strong Ties in an Emerging Industry: Experimental Evidence of the Effects of Status Hierarchies and Personal Ties in Venture Capitalist Decision Making focus on cognitive bias in investment decision-making.
In Skirting the Issues, Bigelow, et. al., (2014) test whether investor perceptions of entrepreneurial firms at the final stage of the entrepreneurial finance life cycle—the initial public offering (IPO)—are shaped by the gender demographics of the top management team. We do this by producing a simulated IPO tombstone document where we manipulate the composition of the top management team, and provide these manipulated tombstones to a group of MBAs who then evaluate the offering. Although we imagined that the top management team demographic mix would matter, we found that the only management team attribute that influenced evaluations was the gender of the CEO. We find that female CEOs are disproportionately disadvantaged in their ability to attract growth capital when all other factors are controlled. Despite identical personal qualifications and firm financials, firms led by females were seen as having a poorer strategic position than those led by males, female Founder/CEOs were perceived as less capable than their male counterparts, and IPOs led by female Founder/CEOs were considered less attractive investments. This paper has been widely featured in the press and widely cited as evidence for a significant problem in entrepreneurship: a gender-based capital gap for new ventures.
The Strength of Strong Ties uses an adaptive choice-based conjoint experiment on investment decision-making using a sample of venture capitalists in the United States and Europe to examine how social ties influence early-stage investment. Prior work has identified two ways that social ties influence investment decision-making: directly through social ties, and indirectly through status hierarchies. That work examines these mechanisms independently, and this paper conducts a joint test. Our results reveal that personal ties are more important in early-stage investment decisions, and this effect increases when investing in new market segments. Building on our main result, we also show that European venture investors value the status of other venture capital firms investing in a deal over personal relationships, in contrast to more densely networked US investors. We also find evidence of heterogeneity in how venture capitalists at different stages of their career evaluate the signals generated by other prestigious venture capital firms investing in (or passing on) a deal, compared to relying on their personal network. Although this paper advances the literature in entrepreneurship on investment decision-making by jointly testing different mechanisms, perhaps its most important finding is experimental evidence revealing significant partner-specific heterogeneity in early-stage investment choices, underscoring the importance of specific partners in venture capital firms, rather than the venture capital firm itself.
Relationship Banking and Conflicts of Interest: Evidence from German Initial Public Offerings (Journal of Corporate Finance) continues the exploration of the final stage of the entrepreneurial finance life cycle. Klein, Wuebker, & Zoeller (2016) consider how an entrepreneurial firm should approach the going-public process. The ongoing consolidation of the financial services industry has put universal banks—financial intermediaries that perform both commercial and investment banking—under scrutiny. Advocates argue that entrepreneurial firms benefit from universal banking because the monitoring function of the banks improves the quality of the firm and the decision of the bank to underwrite the IPO serves to certify the startup. On the other hand, critics (including early investors and startups) have argued that universal or “relationship” banking actually promotes conflict of interest. This paper compares the impact of universal banking in its double role of lender and underwriter by comparing universal-bank-underwritten initial public offerings with those written by specialized banks. Results reveal that universal-bank affiliation is correlated with higher first-day returns, suggesting evidence of underpricing. However, these early returns are uncorrelated with long-term performance, suggesting that underpricing compensates for potential conflicts of interest. We also find evidence that preexisting bank relationships, rather than issuer characteristics, determine an entrepreneurial firm’s choice of underwriter. These findings provide support for the position that choosing a lender (and, ultimately underwriter) is an important strategic decision for the stakeholders governing the startup’s going-public process.
Combining data on corporate venture capital investment and data on lawsuits between corporate venture capital investors and startups, Is a Reputation for Misconduct Harmful? Evidence From Corporate Venture Capital (Journal of Business Research) explores whether a corporate venture capital firm’s reputation for misconduct has any effect on the volume of deal flow the corporate venture capital firm sees. Anokhin, Morgan, Schulze, & Wuebker (2022) decompose the monolithic “firm reputation” construct in prior work into three dimensions particularly salient in a venture capital context—reputation built up through experience and longevity; reputation for the level of involvement with portfolio companies; and reputation for misconduct as reflected in litigation—and reveals that the relationship between a corporate venture capital firm’s reputation and performance is much more nuanced that prior work admits. Perhaps the biggest surprise of this study is that a positive effect of a reputation for misconduct has a positive effect on the firm’s ability to attract promising investments. Being implicated in legal disputes seems to raise the profile of the corporate venture capital firm, increasing the number of potential investments it sees. Our results suggest that, in venture investment, all publicity is good publicity.
Search and learning are central to several literatures in entrepreneurship and strategy. While there is broad agreement that organization of search is a crucial component of value creation, a persistent question emerging from prior work are the conditions under which search processes that privilege information-gathering are superior to strategies that emphasize immediate action and learning-by-doing. While some prior work theorizes as to the contexts in which one or the other approach might be more suitable, the literature has left the question of who entrepreneurs learn from in this process in the shadows, implicitly or explicitly assuming that valuable information is gathered by learning from potential customers. Prediction and Control: An Agent-Based Simulation of Search Processes in the Entrepreneurial Problem Space (Strategic Entrepreneurship Journal) develops an agent-based model simulating the entrepreneurial process that captures some of this nuance. Mauer, Wuebker, Schlüter, and Brettel (2018) populate a simulated world with makers attempting to build valuable offerings, and consumers representing the market for those artifacts. The makers in this world can use one of two processes to create their offerings. The first process focuses on gathering information from consumers. The second eschews market feedback entirely, focusing instead on other agent-makers and attempting to enroll them in co-creating their product or service. We use environmental uncertainty as a shift parameter to explore the boundary conditions of these two archetypal approaches, revealing a much more nuanced relationship between the nature of an idea, environment, and search technique than prior work suggests. This paper continues to influence the scholarly conversation in entrepreneurship on entrepreneurial search, and one particular result—the insight that, sometimes, it may be optimal to ignore customer feedback and develop directly—casts doubt on the naive view of search and experimentation proffered in some prior work.
Heisenberg Effects In Experimentation On Business Ideas (Academy of Management Review, revise and resubmit) extends some of these insights on search and learning, connecting them to the growing literature on business experimentation. Much of the prior work on business experimentation assumes that the choice about when and how to experiment is, ultimately, a trade-off between the cost of an experiment and the value of the information it reveals. Actors experiment to gain information about the value of an idea until the cost of that experimentation exceeds its value (when to experiment) and actors select between different experimental techniques based on their ability to provide the most information at the least cost (how to experiment). Shelef, Wuebker, and Barney (2020) draw from disparate work in economics, competitive strategy, and entrepreneurship to show that it is possible that experimentation can also change the value of an idea—we call these “Heisenberg Effects”. We then develop a formal model that shows that the presence of Heisenberg effects upends the recommendations in prior work about when and how to experiment. For example, adding Heisenberg effects to the analysis suggests that, sometimes, rational actors will not engage in highly informative business experiments that are also low cost—even choosing to run a costly experiment that provides little to no information about the value of an idea. Further, the presence of Heisenberg effects also changes how an actor selects between different experimental techniques. Sometimes, rational actors will choose a more costly and less informative experimental technique in order to optimize the net impacts of Heisenberg effects associated with the experiment. One of the surprising findings in this paper is that the range of ideas for which actors optimally elect to not experiment, and instead develop directly and eschew market feedback, is surprisingly large. Although the current trend in the fields of entrepreneurship and the broader business literature is to privilege experimentation and to make product development “more scientific”, the findings in this paper suggest that, for a wide range of possible ideas, experimentation can be suboptimal or even harmful.
My more recent research on the entrepreneurial process has shifted from a narrow focus on an archetypal or simulated economic actor to the new venture team and, more broadly, the role of stakeholders in the entrepreneurial process. I have several projects, and one exploratory paper, that relaxes the assumption of the individual actor, as I start to take seriously the notion of an entrepreneurial process with the entrepreneurial team occupying center stage.
My research on entrepreneurial teams started with several bouts of fieldwork—in large measure thanks to financial support from the Eccles School and a Strategic Management Society Research in Organizations Grant (2019). During the COVID-19 travel lockdowns, I also sought out and started to remotely collaborate with scholars focused on “isolated, confined, and extreme” (ICE) teams. Nathan Smith (University of Coventry) and I now have two exploratory empirical projects (one with NASA with teams simulating off-world conditions, and one with teams of Arctic explorers) that are in process and target specialist journals.
Along with these exploratory forays that directly contribute to conversations within the “extreme teams” literature, Entrepreneurial Teams and Individual Well-Being (forthcoming, Review de Entrepreneuriat Special Issue: Entrepreneurship and Well-Being) explicitly connects the literature on extreme teams to a growing literature on entrepreneurial well-being. Smith, Thiel, and Wuebker (2022) contribute to the literature on well-being in entrepreneurship by developing theory and hypotheses about how individual self-regulation strategies and team dynamics interact to shape entrepreneurial outcomes.
Organizations, Entrepreneurship and Strategy
The remainder of my research focuses on how organizations shape innovative activity and the entrepreneurial process.
At inception, every organizational form is an innovation. Some scholars argue that management innovation, more than any other kind of innovation, has allowed companies to cross new performance thresholds. This raises the question: how can we assess the value of a new organizational form? Evaluating an Organizational Innovation: Evidence From the Conglomerate Merger Wave (Industrial and Corporate Change) (Klein, Wuebker, Chen, and Zoeller, 2021) apply an experimental-learning framework to explore how the capital markets evaluate an organizational innovation using the conglomerate merger wave in the late 1960s and 1970s as an empirical test-bed. Event-study results suggest that the initial market popularity of conglomerates, followed by their rapid decline, is indicative of investors attempting to determine the value of a new organizational form. We show that over time, investors systematically updated their beliefs about the value of the conglomerate form as a whole based on new information—positive and negative—about the largest and most newsworthy conglomerates. This paper provides evidence that financial market participants make judgements not only about specific companies, but about organizational structure itself.
Corporate Diversification and Innovation: Managerial Myopia or Inefficient Internal Capital Markets? (Managerial and Decision Economics) tackles another longstanding question at the intersection of entrepreneurship, innovation, and organizations: which is more innovative, the decentralized, diversified large organization or a smaller, more narrowly focused entrepreneurial firm? Some argue that large firms have natural innovation advantages because managers of these firms have access to an internal capital market. In contrast, others maintain that managers of incumbent firms suffer from “managerial myopia” which prevents them from investing in projects with long-term, uncertain payoffs. Klein and Wuebker (2020) take a fresh look at the innovation and diversification question using a comprehensive sample of diversified and non-diversified firms and an analytical approach that teases out the mechanisms shaping the innovation-diversification relationship. Results reveal a robust and negative correlation between diversification and innovation, along with evidence that diversification reduces innovation by discouraging investment. However, the analysis also suggests that internal capital market inefficiencies, not managerial myopia, is the mechanism responsible for the negative relationship between diversification and innovation.
Organizations have long attempted to capture insights from individuals outside the firm boundary, for example harnessing highly engaged customers, holding innovation contests, and collaborating on open-source projects. Unsurprisingly, there is a longstanding and active literature on dispersed, crowd-powered innovation through mechanisms such as incentive prizes, crowdsourcing, and (in recent years) blockchain governance. Unfortunately, theory development has often lagged descriptions of these various mechanisms, and the managerial recommendations are diverse and often contradictory. When, where and under what conditions does a diffuse or open approach to innovation make sense, and given the decision to engage in distributed innovation, how should managers choose between particular approaches? In Problems, Theories, and Governing the Crowd (Strategic Organization), Nickerson, Wuebker, and Zenger (2017) argue that this question is, fundamentally, a design problem: economic actors can successfully catalyze innovation when they efficiently match attributes of the problem or domain of problems ripe for innovation to different modes of solution search. The paper argues for the central role of comparative governance in deciding how to organize innovative activity generally, and distributed innovation in particular. Fundamentally, a theory of distributed innovation requires the ability to understand its costs and benefits, when an economic actor would choose it over alternative forms of organization, and the factors that, on the margin, affect its boundary conditions.
Do firms facing a sudden, sharp downturn in demand respond by innovating, or doubling down and doing more of what they already know how to do? In The More Things Change, The More They Stay the Same: Demand-Side Responses to Economic Shocks (Managerial and Decision Economics), Knudsen, Lien, Timmermans, and Wuebker (2021) examine this question using a unique survey of Norwegian firms during the 2008 capital markets crisis, in an attempt to better understand how firms respond to a particular organizational dislocation—a sudden and swift reduction in demand—and how firms with different capabilities or operating in different contexts develop their strategic responses. Results broadly align with theories of path dependency and inertia/rigidities in the strategy literature—the firms in our sample were more likely to respond to a recessionary shock by implementing strategic changes that reinforced whatever resources and capabilities they were exploiting before the shock. Although there is a broad portfolio of possible responses available to firms, in practice the firms in our sample seem to be choosing from a menu of strategic options that preserves consistency with their pre-recession strategy.
Scholars have long been interested in Schumpeterian competition, and in particular how both potential and realized entry shape the strategic choices of incumbent firms. Prior work shows that the threat of entry induces some incumbents to erect barriers to deter it from occurring and/or to make pre-emptive investments in its resources and capabilities in case deterrence fails. Much less attention has been paid in the literature to how managers should respond in the midst of competition with a potentially disruptive entrepreneurial entrant. Even the theory exploring how incumbents respond to entry is fragmented across several literatures. How Should An Incumbent Respond To An Entrepreneurial Entrant? (Under Review, Strategy Science), Shelef, Wuebker, Saouma and McGahan (2022) contribute to the literature on disruptive competition and the adjacent literature on entry dynamics in strategy by developing a framework of responses available to incumbent firms facing an entrepreneurial entrant. A central contribution of the paper is that it permits the joint analysis of a range of possible strategies, and details when each of these strategies will be more, or less, effective. Crucially, insights revealed from an analysis of the framework overturn some of the recommendations found in prior work, and broaden the range of optimal responses by incumbent firms to a new entrant. This paper shows that an incumbent can make strategic choices that leverage its legacy assets strategically—both before and after entry—to shape the competitive terrain such that the potentially disruptive entrant is compelled to compete on terrain more favorable to the incumbent.
Teaching
Introduction
The courses I teach at the David Eccles School of Business, the Multi-Disciplinary Design Program, and the College of Engineering represent an instantiation of a particular (and evolving) point of view about entrepreneurship and the entrepreneurial process—one collectively generated by myself and my colleagues at the Eccles School. Over the years, my classroom has become a proving ground for innovative teaching approaches and measuring outcomes that demonstrate the efficacy and relevance of our Department’s overall approach.
I have designed and taught several entrepreneurship courses within the Eccles School in the Undergraduate and MBA programs since arriving at The University of Utah as a postdoctoral fellow. I also contributed to the development of Entrepreneurship Major and Entrepreneurship Minor offered through the Eccles School. In that role I assisted with sourcing, onboarding, and training adjunct faculty, eventually transitioning several courses that I designed and taught to other faculty members. I assisted the College of Engineering and the College of Architecture and Planning in the development of courses targeting undergraduate and graduate engineers (Engineering Entrepreneurship Certificate) and undergraduate and graduate designers (Multi-Disciplinary Design Major).
As the arc of my research suggests, teaching students has significantly contributed to the evolution of my scholarly activities. It’s a recursive process: my own research (often re-constituted as an essay or teaching note), along with work produced by my colleagues, often features as preparation material in class. Where there is a dearth of relevant or accessible class preparation material I create my own essay and publish it internally, and if it has broad appeal I publish it on outlets like Substack or Medium. Some of these essays end up evolving into projects and/or working papers. Others have been picked up and distributed in other entrepreneurship courses at the Eccles School and elsewhere. Further, a volume at the intersection of renewable energy and entrepreneurship that I co-produced as a part of a grant from the Swiss National Science Foundation, Handbook of Research on Energy Entrepreneurship (Wüstenhagen and Wuebker, 2011) has been adopted by some courses as a textbook and teaching guide.
It also seems that I have a knack for being around when motivated students with entrepreneurial intentions launch impactful businesses. Over the years, I have had many opportunities to explore and refine my understanding of how this can be accomplished in the classroom, directly; through experienced-based educational interventions such as The Foundry, a novel approach to accelerating student startups and short, intense “boot camps” for founders and founding teams delivered at the University of St. Gallen and elsewhere. In both my undergraduate and graduate entrepreneurship courses, interested individuals routinely stand up new businesses and/or accelerate their progress on an idea or proto-business they have been working on. Some of these student experiences become lore, and their launch experiences feed directly back into the classroom. I view one of the trailing indicators of value created through my classes is the willingness of former participants to return to the class and share their stories and ever-evolving insights as speakers and panelists. An important element in many of my courses is “real time cases”—founding teams come back to speak, often with guests such as their investors in tow, and discuss the state of the business and engage in dialogue with myself and my class about what’s next.
My courses and teaching have had some influence on entrepreneurship education outside the University of Utah. For example, the entrepreneurship programs that I helped to design and produce, including the Foundry, were recognized in 2011 with a USASBE Excellence in Entrepreneurship Education award (USASBE is the largest independent, professional, academic organization in the world dedicated to advancing the discipline of entrepreneurship). At the University of Utah I have received the Dean’s Teaching Award in the College of Engineering for every year that I have taught in the program. I have also had the good fortune to speak on entrepreneurship policy and entrepreneurship education at the request of public and private-sector institutions—for example, as a delegate and speaker at the Global Entrepreneurship Conference. The Foundry in particular, and its (for the time) novel approach to entrepreneurship education that emphasized basic business skill-building and peer coaching, has taken root all over the world—in part because of an early decision to offer all of the playbooks and technology associated with standing up a Foundry through a Creative Commons Attribution license.
The original Foundry program received substantial media coverage during its origins (national coverage included The Wall Street Journal, Fast Company, Bloomberg and Businessweek). The Foundry model now has a life of its own, and the program/program components have found their way into universities, business incubators, and venture capital-backed accelerators around the world. The educational components the Foundry uses are all Creative Commons licensed and have received over 1,000,000 views on YouTube.

Paper Summaries
Heisenberg Effects In Experimentation On Business Ideas (Working Paper)
Much of the prior work on business experimentation assumes that the choice about when and how to experiment on a business idea is, ultimately, a trade-off between the cost of an experiment and the value of the information it reveals. This paper captures the possibility that experimentation can also change the value of an idea —Heisenberg effects—in a formal model. Heisenberg effects often overturn the recommendations of prior work about when and how to experiment. For example, prior work suggests that actors should experiment when an experiment is informative and low cost. Adding Heisenberg effects to the analysis suggests that, sometimes, rational actors will not engage in highly informative experiments that are also low cost—even choosing costly experiments that provide little to no information about the value of an idea. Prior work also offers guidance for selecting between experimental techniques, arguing that rational actors choose a technique that maximizes information and minimizes cost. Adding Heisenberg effects to the analysis suggests that, sometimes, rational actors will choose more costly and less informative experimental techniques in order to optimize the net impacts of Heisenberg effects associated with the experiment.
Entrepreneurial Theories (Working Paper)
Entrepreneurs face a host of early choices with significant long-term consequences for the ventures they found. Entrepreneurs must decide what form of financing to use, which assets to purchase or employ, whether to keep their ideas secret or not, and what type of experimentation to engage in. This paper argues that these and many of the choices entrepreneurs face in the earliest stages of the entrepreneurial process depend critically on the nature of the entrepreneurial idea or theory they plan to pursue. This paper develops a contingent theory that enables entrepreneurs to appropriately match their theories of value with the appropriate forms of entrepreneurial action.
How Should An Incumbent Respond To An Entrepreneurial Entrant? (Working Paper)
Much of the prior scholarly  work on incumbent responses to an entrepreneurial entrant has a “time machine” flavor to it. Essentially, the field of strategy suggests that the best response for managers of incumbent firms facing an entrepreneurial entrant is to run it back and make better choices: a more flexible organizational form, more effective pre-emptive investments, et cetera. Insofar as academics consider the question of what a manager of an incumbent firm should do in the midst of competition with a new entrant, the advice on offer remains mostly generic templates—attack, compete, ignore, retreat—with no clear guidance about how to choose between them. Consequently, prior work offers a paucity of applicable insight as to how an incumbent firm should respond to an entrepreneurial entrant, nor insight about when a given response may be more or less valuable. This paper develops a framework of responses available to incumbent firms facing an entrant that may eventually grow to become an existential threat, and details when each of these strategies will be more, or less, effective. Crucially, insights revealed from an analysis of the framework upend some of the recommendations found in prior work, and broaden the range of optimal responses by incumbent firms to a new entrant.
Entrepreneurial Teams And Individual Well-Being (forthcoming, Revue de l'Entrepreneuriat)
Why do some individuals thrive during the process of new venture creation, while others experience the dark side of entrepreneurship? Building on self-determination theory and recent research on extreme teams, this paper argues that team dynamics are a critical, but relatively understudied, input to individual well-being in entrepreneurial settings. Entrepreneurship is most often a team sport, and this paper contributes to a growing literature on entrepreneurial well-being by exploring how individual self-regulation strategies and team dynamics interact to sculpt entrepreneurial outcomes.
Is A Reputation For Misconduct Harmful? Evidence From Corporate Venture Capital (Journal of Business Research)
This paper explores how three distinct types of reputation—experience, involvement, and misconduct—influence a corporate venture capital firm’s ability to attract potential investments. Combining data on corporate venture capital firms with detailed information about lawsuits filed against those firms, we find that in our sample of corporate venture capital firms and startups the relationship between a firm’s reputation and performance is much more nuanced than prior work suggests. Perhaps the biggest surprise of this study is the positive effect of a reputation for misconduct on the ability of the corporate venture capital firm to attract promising startups. Being implicated in legal disputes seems to raise the profile of the corporate venture capital firm, increasing the number of potential investments it sees.
Evaluating an Organizational Innovation: Evidence From The Conglomerate Merger Wave (Industrial and Corporate Change)
At inception, every new organizational form is an innovation, and its value is largely unknown. How do we assess the value of new forms of economic organization? This paper applies an experimental-learning framework to explore how capital markets evaluate an organizational innovation using the conglomerate merger wave in the late 1960s and 1970s as an empirical setting. Results suggest that the initial market popularity of conglomerates, followed by their rapid decline, is indicative of investors attempting to determine the value of a new organizational form. We show that over time, investors systematically updated their beliefs about the value of the conglomerate form as a whole based on new information—positive and negative—about the largest and most newsworthy conglomerates. Our results suggest that financial market participants make judgements not only about specific companies, but about organizational structure itself.
The More Things Change, The More They Stay The Same: Firm Demand-Side Responses to Economic Shocks (Managerial and Decision Economics)
How firms should optimally respond to economic crises, in particular sudden reductions in demand, is a question both scholars and managers care about. This paper leverages a unique survey of Norwegian firms during the 2008 recession to better understand how firms with different capabilities and operating in different contexts develop their strategic responses to demand shocks. While some prior work has isolated and studied a particular type of response to a recession, a central contribution of this paper is the joint consideration of the broad portfolio of possible responses, and how and why firms choose between those responses. Our analysis offers insight into the heterogeneity of firm responses to an economic crisis, and important antecedents such as firm-specific capabilities that drive those decisions.
Corporate Diversification And Innovation: Managerial Myopia, Or Inefficient Internal Capital Markets? (Managerial and Decision Economics)
Which is more innovative: the decentralized, diversified large organization or a smaller, more narrowly focused entrepreneurial firm? This paper takes a fresh look at the innovation and diversification question using a comprehensive sample of diversified and non-diversified firms and a novel analytical approach that teases out the mechanisms shaping the innovation-diversification relationship. Results reveal a robust and negative correlation between diversification and innovation, along with evidence that diversification reduces innovation by discouraging investment. However, the analysis also suggests that internal capital market inefficiencies, not managerial myopia, is primarily responsible for the negative relationship between diversification and innovation.
Social Objectivity And Entrepreneurial Opportunities (Academy of Management Journal)
Entrepreneurial opportunities, along with everything else management scholars study, involves social entities—so it is critically important that the field understand what a social entity is and what makes social institutions like markets, organizations, and profit opportunities real and objective. This paper shows that a clearer understanding of the nature of social institutions resolves several ongoing puzzles about the nature of entrepreneurial opportunity—one of the field’s most pressing and important questions— and offers a new perspective from which to understand the nature of value and the particulars of the entrepreneurial process.
An Economic Model Of Strategic Entrepreneurship (Strategic Entrepreneurship Journal)
Although there is broad consensus that the essence of entrepreneurship is the creation and capture of new economic value, scholars have struggled to convert this central axiom to the particulars of the entrepreneurial process. This paper offers a formal model of entrepreneurial entry grounded in neoclassical economics, modeling entry and competition as occurring simultaneously in production technology space and product attribute space. A central feature of the model is to disaggregate the eponymous ‘entrepreneurial rent’ into four potential and distinct rent streams: arbitrage, innovation, organization and uncertainty-bearing. Examining these mechanisms together reveals implications for how best to organize value creation and capture throughout the entrepreneurial process, and to conceptualize value capture as a set of distinct rent streams available to multiple stakeholders over time.
Prediction And Control: An Agent-Based Simulation Of The Entrepreneurial Problem Space (Strategic Entrepreneurship Journal)
Search and learning are central to the entrepreneurial process, and a longstanding question in the entrepreneurship literature are the conditions under which search processes that privilege information-gathering are superior to strategies that emphasize immediate action and learning-by-doing. Prior theoretical work underpinning these two perspectives tends to take the environment as given and immutable, differing only in their recommendations for how to search given an unchanging environment. This paper contributes to a growing body of work in strategy and entrepreneurship that challenges this environment-as-given assumption, developing a simulated world (an agent-based model) as a laboratory to examine the entrepreneurial process. We populate this world with makers attempting to build valuable offerings, and consumers representing the market for those artifacts. The makers in this world can use one of two processes to create their offerings. The first process focuses on gathering information from consumers. The second eschews market feedback entirely, focusing instead on other agent-makers and attempting to enroll them in co-creating their product or service. We use environmental uncertainty as a shift parameter to explore the boundary conditions of these two archetypal approaches, revealing a much more nuanced relationship between the nature of an idea, environment, and search technique than suggested by prior work.
Problems, Theories, And Governing the Crowd (Strategic Organization)
When, where and under what conditions does a diffuse or open approach to innovation make sense, and given the decision to engage in distributed innovative activity, how should managers choose between particular approaches? Much of the prior work on crowd-powered innovation through mechanisms such as crowdsourcing or blockchain governance is diverse and often contradictory. This paper argues that this question is, fundamentally, a design problem: economic actors can successfully catalyze innovation when they efficiently match attributes of the problem or domain of problems ripe for innovation to different modes of solution search. This paper argues for the central role of comparative governance in deciding how to organize innovative activity generally, and distributed innovation in particular. Fundamentally, a theory of distributed innovation requires the ability to understand its costs and benefits, when an economic actor would choose it over alternative forms of organization, and the factors that, on the margin, affect its boundary conditions.
On Opportunities: Philosophical And Empirical Implications (Academy of Management Review)
The field of entrepreneurship has been obsessed with whether entrepreneurial opportunities are created or discovered for almost two decades. Although the question can at times seem esoteric and impractical, the implications for research and practice are actually quite profound, which in part explains the sustained interest in the topic. Scholars of the “discovery view” of entrepreneurial opportunity assert that opportunities are objective and real, and in their search for a coherent intellectual foundation for this perspective have drawn from philosophy’s toolkit to position the “opportunity discovery versus opportunity creation” debate within a broader debate about critical realism and social constructionism. We show that adopting a critical realist perspective to analyze the nature of opportunity is unsuccessful at resolving a series of internal contradictions, and generates implications that are unpalatable. Further, we argue that the debate about critical realism and social constructionism is itself suspect, resulting from a basic misunderstanding and resulting mishandling of both perspectives.
Relationship Banking And Conflicts Of Interest: Evidence From German IPOs (Journal of Corporate Finance)
Continued consolidation of the financial services industry has focused attention on universal banks—financial intermediaries that perform both commercial and investment banking. Some have argued that while universal banks can, in principle, improve the quality of corporate governance through the monitoring of their client companies. Critics argue that universal or “relationship” banking actually promotes conflict of interest. This paper compares the impact of universal banking in its double role of lender and underwriter by comparing universal-bank-underwritten initial public offerings with those written by specialized banks. If investors perceive universal banks as underwriters with conflicts of interest, then lower initial prices could be compensation for the potential hazards associated with the underwriter’s pre-existing lending relationships. Alternatively, investors might perceive universal banks as certifiers of high quality issues, which would encourage higher offer prices. Results reveal that universal-bank affiliation is correlated with higher first-day returns, suggesting evidence of underpricing. However, these early returns are uncorrelated with long-term performance, suggesting that underpricing compensates for potential conflicts of interest. Our results also suggest that preexisting bank relationships, rather than issuer characteristics, determine an entrepreneurial firm’s choice of underwriter.
Bricolage, Effectuation, And Opportunity Creation (Strategic Entrepreneurship Journal)
Opportunity creation, effectuation, and bricolage are three related theories in entrepreneurship, each describing how entrepreneurs create new economic value through action. Although prior work often conceptualizes these concepts as interrelated, precisely how they relate to and compliment one another and where they diverge remains unclear. This paper examines the roots of each of these concepts and their underlying assumptions and organizes them within a unifying conceptual frame. Analysis of this framework reveals a set of entailing implications that can guide future work in entrepreneurship, and advances our collective  understanding of value creation and capture in strategy, entrepreneurship, and organization theory.
Realism In The Study Of Entrepreneurship (Academy of Management Review)
Arguably, the single overarching problem in the social sciences has been finding suitable intellectual foundations for them. The question that torments social scientists is: how is it possible that we seem to uncover new objective facts amidst social structures that are subjective and man-made? In other words, how is it possible to make ‘objective’ discoveries in the field of management, or to talk about the ‘objectivity’ of entrepreneurial opportunity, in systems that are distinctly different from the natural world? This article argues that the underlying intellectual foundations in management scholarship broadly, and the field of entrepreneurship in particular, are indeed fundamentally flawed, but that the threat is neither inextricable nor existential. The theories that scholars in the social sciences have borrowed to buttress their theorizing in prior work have been supplanted by a host of new candidate theories. We detail one of these theories—social ontology—and show how it can be fruitfully applied to the question of created versus discovered opportunities in entrepreneurship.
The Strength of Strong Ties In An Emerging Industry: A Joint Test For The Effects of Status Hierarchies and Personal Ties In Venture Capitalist Decision-Making (Strategic Entrepreneurship Journal)
Prior work in entrepreneurial finance has identified two ways in which social ties influence venture capital investment decisions: directly through personal ties and indirectly through status hierarchies. Previous research has examined these effects independently. Our study is the first to perform a joint examination of the role of social ties and status hierarchies in venture capital decision-making. We examine the relative importance of these two mechanisms through an adaptive choice-based conjoint experiment comprising 3,132 investment decisions made by 86 venture capitalists from the United States and Europe. Our findings reveal that personal ties are more important in venture capital decision-making when compared to the relative status of other venture capital firms participating in the investment syndicate, and this effect increases as investment uncertainty increases. Building on our main findings, we show that the influence of personal ties is less pronounced in the European investment community, as compared to more densely networked U.S. investors. We also find a U-shaped relationship between venture capitalist experience and the influence of personal networks on investment decisions.
Skirting the Issues? Experimental Evidence Of Gender Bias In IPO Prospectus Evaluations (Journal of Management)
Given that women executives are present in the top management teams of IPO firms in increasing numbers, the lack of female-led entrepreneurial firms navigating the going-public process is puzzling—especially since women-owned private businesses represent almost half of the new businesses formed in the United States, with patterns of founding similar to male-owned businesses. These facts hint at a potentially larger problem: a gender-based financing gap for privately-held firms. We test whether investor perceptions are aligned with these empirical patterns by constructing a simulated initial public offering tombstone, manipulating the gender demographics of the top management team, and using a sample of MBA students to evaluate the offering. Our results show that female CEOs are disproportionately disadvantaged in their ability to attract growth capital when all other factors are controlled. Despite identical personal qualifications and firm financials, firms led by females were seen as having a poorer strategic position than those led by males, female Founder/CEOs were perceived as less capable than their male counterparts, and IPOs led by female Founder/CEOs were considered less attractive investments.
Exploring Country-Level Institutional Arrangements On The Rate And Type Of Entrepreneurial Activity (Journal of Business Venturing)
There is continued interest by policymakers in stimulating entrepreneurial activity. Despite decades of sustained scholarly inquiry, we still struggle to explain why rates of entrepreneurial activity vary widely across regions and countries. Traditional explanations have tended to focus on the influence of entrepreneurial framework conditions on new venture creation, arguing that the presence and strength of these conditions explain observed differences in the rate and type of entrepreneurial activity. Unfortunately, reality did not get the memo. Empirical tests of this conjecture have produced mixed, muddled results and attempts to implement ‘entrepreneur-friendly’ policies in nations, regions, and cities have produced unexpected, undesired outcomes. This paper contributes to the literature on institutions and entrepreneurship by showing that various framework conditions and policy have differential effects on the rate of entrepreneurial activity and what type of entrepreneurial firms emerge. Crucially, we show that policies and institutional support structures that focus on high-growth entrepreneurship can suppress more ordinary, workaday entrepreneurial activity and that policy interventions that attempt to promote entrepreneurship more broadly can suppress the formation and growth of high impact entrepreneurial firms. We find support for our conjectures using the Global Entrepreneurship Monitor dataset, using a novel multidimensional measure of a country’s entrepreneurial environment.
Strategic Alliances In Venture-Backed Firms: An Empirical Examination (Small Business Economics)
A growing literature examines the formation of strategic alliances as an important value-added role provided by venture capital firms. This paper contributes to this literature by examining two related questions: whether venture capital firms use strategic alliances as a substitute or compliment to capital infusion, and how venture capital firms use alliances as a means to mitigate different types of risk. Results from a sample of 2,505 venture-backed startups reveal that venture capital firms treat alliance formation as a substitute for capital infusion, and that the breadth of the network of syndication partners investing in the startup increases the number of its strategic alliances. We also find intentionality in alliance formation. Specifically, venture-backed firms operating in industry environments characterized by technical risk are more likely to form alliances with partners capable of mitigating technical risks; and firms operating in environments characterized by market risk are more likely to form alliances with partners capable of mitigating market risk. Our findings lend additional support to the perspective that alliances represent an important mechanism through which venture capital firms add value to their portfolio companies.
Does State Of Incorporation Matter For New Ventures? Evidence From Venture Capital Investment And The Going-Public Process (Venture Capital)
In the United States, entrepreneurial firms choose the state in which they incorporate, and are subject to the laws of the state in which they are incorporated. Incorporating in Delaware is a common move for startups, especially those interested in attracting venture capital. Using a large sample of privately-held companies, we empirically investigate the implications of Delaware incorporation and examine its effect on access to venture capital financing and the going-public process. Results suggest that companies incorporated in Delaware receive more venture financing and attract more involvement from different venture capitalists than startups incorporated elsewhere. In addition, Delaware incorporated venture-backed firms are more likely to go public, get to a liquidity event faster, and generate more exit value (IPO proceeds and acquisition value) than similar firms incorporated elsewhere. Overall, this study reveals the first empirical evidence about the importance of state laws to privately held, informationally opaque firms seeking venture capital support.
Projects
The Foundry
In Spring 2010, participants in my evening ENTP 5770 - Venture Foundations course began to reassemble after class at the Lassonde Entrepreneur Institute to harvest free pizza (provided by Gangplank Utah and the Lassonde Entrepreneur Center) and continue to work on their nascent startups late into the night. As the semester wound down, a core group of motivated participants proposed a “Startup Summer Camp”—essentially, continuing the course beyond the semester’s end.
After unsuccessfully attempting to talk the class out of destroying my plans to focus on perfecting my tan, I suggested a Summer curriculum where I taught them things, lecture style—perfectly on-brand for a novice professor—which they roundly and wisely rejected. What they envisioned in its place, and ultimately invented, was the first iteration of the Foundry.
The Foundry has evolved from its scrappy roots in boosted space in downtown Salt Lake City and is now embedded in and supported by what is now the Lassonde Entrepreneur Institute. Thanks to the original “open source” approach of the early Foundry, institutions and universities all over the world continue to contribute insights to this ever-evolving entrepreneurship “education” program.
Entrepreneurial Solutions to Climate Challenges
The University of St. Gallen has hosted several iterations of various Masters’ courses from me at the intersection of entrepreneurship and renewable energy/sustainability. Each iteration was offered “boot camp” style—a three-day blitz. The outcome of the course varied depending on the intentions of the class and the goals of the program in which the course was offered. Some iterations are best described as survey courses that focus on imparting the rudiments of entrepreneurship and the entrepreneurial process to those committed to a career in cleantech, but not sure about where. Other iterations were more “gated entry”, focusing narrowly and exclusively on founders and founding teams who had ideas they were proving out and hoped to build into high impact businesses.
Alumni from these courses have gone on to found and grow companies, take positions leading sustainability initiatives in established firms, or work in private equity/venture capital. None of this would have been possible absent the NSF IGERT fellowship that catalyzed  my original interest in this space; a Swiss National Science Foundation grant (100014_125044 / 1) and ThinkSwiss grant, which accelerated several research and educational collaborations; and the University of St. Gallen, which offered support, encouragement, and a platform for innovation and risk-taking.

We need to imagine and then install a new future for food. Working with the Basque Culinary Center is a way that I can learn how to contribute in some small way to the creation of that future.
Extreme Teams: M.A.R.S. and DRIFT
M.A.R.S. is an interdisciplinary translational research studio led by Dr. Nathan Smith. Nathan leads the workforce resilience and performance program at Coventry University’s Protective Security and Resilience Centre. His research includes studies of people in extreme environments on Earth and individuals and teams preparing for remote and long duration expeditions in space.
The focus of our collaboration is the intersection between research on isolated, confined, and extreme (ICE) teams —which focuses on team performance in contexts like space exploration, special operations, first responders—and, as I argue, entrepreneurial founding teams.
I also contributed to the design and development of the first version of the DRIFT app—an evidence-based digital support system to train and optimize behavior, performance and health in extreme environments—and aspire to use the next version with founding teams.
